GLOBAL

1. Microsoft Tightens Controls on Workplace Speech and Remote Work

Following a protest in which employees occupied an executive's office over the company's contract with the Israeli government, Microsoft has implemented stricter policies on internal communication and workplace access. The company shut down an internal forum where employees could question senior leaders and discuss societal issues, replacing it with a more controlled channel and prioritizing town hall meetings. In addition, Microsoft is now restricting employee access to certain buildings and is requiring all Seattle-area employees to work from the office three days a week, a change from its previous remote-work policy. These actions come after a series of protests against the company's Azure cloud computing contract with Israel, which some employees claim enables the country to harm Palestinians in Gaza.

2. OpenAI Executives Concerned Over Restructuring Opposition

OpenAI executives are reportedly worried that growing political pressure in California could block their plan to transition the company into a for-profit entity. This restructuring is crucial as investors have tied approximately $19 billion in funding to the change, which would give them traditional equity. A coalition of philanthropies, nonprofits, and labor groups is urging the California attorney general to intervene, arguing that the restructuring violates charitable trust laws given the company’s nonprofit origins. Facing this scrutiny, OpenAI has hired advisors to build support in Sacramento and has even discussed the possibility of leaving California as a last resort if regulators complicate the transition. The company's executives were also reportedly surprised by the intense public backlash and the recent letter from the California and Delaware attorneys general, which also raised concerns about AI safety after recent reported suicides linked to ChatGPT interactions.

3. Apple Unveils Thinner iPhone Air to Spur Upgrades Amidst AI Challenges

Apple has launched the new "iPhone Air," an ultrathin, mid-range model priced at $999, to replace its previous Plus models and drive customer upgrades. The new design is seen as a precursor to future foldable phones. While the base iPhone 17 model remains at $799, the cheapest Pro model now has a higher starting price of $1,099. This pricing strategy may help the company offset the impact of tariffs without explicitly citing them. Despite the new hardware, Apple continues to face challenges with its software, particularly in artificial intelligence, where it lags behind rivals like Google. The company also announced new Apple Watch and AirPods models, and is seeing its stock prices near 2025 highs following favorable developments regarding tariffs and an antitrust case.

INDIA

1. India to Revamp Iron Ore Auctions to Boost Domestic Production

India is overhauling its iron ore auction system to increase domestic output and curb cheap steel imports. The new plan will prioritize a combination of upfront payments and a capped premium of 50%, moving away from the current system where awards are based solely on the highest premium offered. This change aims to make mining more economically viable and encourage companies to start production, as a large number of previously auctioned mines remain non-operational due to high premiums. Additionally, the government is considering a uniform export duty on all grades of iron ore and may introduce minimum dispatch norms for non-auctioned mines to ensure they also contribute to the domestic supply. These reforms are part of a broader effort to reduce input costs for steelmakers and help them compete with global rivals.

2. Brookfield Executive Sees More Opportunities in India's Renewables Space

Nawal Saini, a managing partner at Brookfield Asset Management, stated that India's renewable energy sector has significant potential for creating new "platform assets." Brookfield, which has already committed $3 billion in equity to India’s renewables, is actively exploring opportunities in areas like green mobility to develop another platform soon. Saini’s comments follow the 2023 launch of Evren, a joint venture focused on developing a large pipeline of wind and solar projects. He highlighted that India is a top destination for global investors due to its scale, supportive ecosystem, and "respect for capital," adding that the company plans to increase its sourcing from India.

3. Indian VC Firms Shift Focus to Specialty Healthtech with AI and IP

Venture capital investors in India are increasingly pivoting away from general healthcare startups like digital pharmacies and toward specialized healthtech firms that leverage artificial intelligence (AI) and proprietary intellectual property (IP). This shift is driven by the potential for high value creation and the possibility of future acquisition by large pharmaceutical companies. While earlier investments heavily favored companies like PharmEasy, recent funding has flowed into niche areas such as AI-powered data analytics (Innovaccer), AI-based diagnostics (Qure.ai), stem cell therapy (Eyestem), and medical devices. Despite a host of challenges, including the need for long-term capital and complex regulatory approvals, investors are betting that these specialized startups will be able to capitalize on India's vast dataset and create scalable, high-impact solutions.
