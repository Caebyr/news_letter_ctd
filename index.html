GLOBAL

1. The Unexpected Rise in AI Costs

Despite predictions that AI would become cheaper, companies that create apps and services using cutting-edge AI models are facing higher-than-expected costs. This is because while the price per token, the unit of AI, has dropped, the newest models are so-called "reasoning" more, requiring a significantly larger number of tokens to complete complex tasks like writing sophisticated code or analyzing legal documents. This shift has led to a debate about who will win and lose in the AI market, with some startups like Notion and Replit seeing their profit margins impacted and even adjusting their pricing models. As a result, some in the industry are suggesting that businesses and consumers may need to consider using cheaper, less powerful AI models for simpler tasks to manage expenses.

2. Kraft Heinz to Split into Two Separate Companies

Kraft Heinz, a packaged-food giant, has announced plans to split its business into two distinct companies, reversing the 2015 merger of Kraft and Heinz. One company will focus on global "taste elevation" brands like Heinz ketchup and Philadelphia cream cheese, while the other will manage North American grocery staples, including Oscar Mayer and Lunchables. This strategic move aims to simplify the business and allow for a more focused investment in each brand, following years of flagging share prices and weakened demand for some core products. The decision reflects a broader trend in the food industry away from large-scale mergers and has drawn criticism from major shareholder Warren Buffett, who views the split as costly and disruptive without creating value.

3. Nvidia's Stock Falls After Underwhelming Forecast, Despite Record Sales

Nvidia reported record-breaking sales, reaching $46.7 billion in the last quarter, driven by strong demand for its AI chips in data centers. However, the company's stock fell nearly 3% after it issued a lukewarm revenue forecast of $54 billion for the next quarter, which slightly missed analyst expectations and raised concerns that the rapid growth in AI chip demand may be plateauing. The data center segment's revenue of $41.1 billion, while up 56% from the previous year, also came in just below analyst projections for the second consecutive quarter. These results and the cautious outlook have led to market jitters about the future trajectory of the world's most valuable public company.

INDIA

1. GST Council Considers Major Overhaul: Tax Cuts on Essentials and Higher Levies on Vices

The Goods and Services Tax (GST) Council is meeting to discuss significant changes to India's indirect tax system, aiming to stimulate the economy through a consumption-led recovery. Key proposals include a tax exemption for health insurance premiums and lowering the tax on numerous common goods like condensed milk, cheese, chocolates, and kitchenware from the 12% or 18% slabs to 5%. In contrast, luxury items and sin goods such as pan masala, tobacco, caffeinated drinks, and high-capacity cars are slated to see their tax rates increase from 28% to a new 40% slab. The reforms also aim to correct the inverted duty structure on products like carpets and textiles to encourage fresh investment, with a focus on simplifying the tax structure and boosting demand.

2. Carmakers in India Tap Social Media Influencers to Drive EV Adoption

As India's electric vehicle (EV) adoption rate lags behind expectations, automakers are increasingly partnering with social media influencers to address consumer concerns and boost sales. By collaborating with trusted creators on platforms like YouTube and Instagram, companies are aiming to debunk common myths about EVs, such as range anxiety and charging infrastructure, through authentic, real-world content like test drives and long-term reviews. This strategy is proving to be more effective than traditional advertising, with EV-related social media posts showing higher audience engagement, as consumers actively seek out information on charging, costs, and ownership experiences from voices they trust.

3. India’s Agriculture Infrastructure Fund Draws Over ₹1.15 Trillion in Investment

India's Agriculture Infrastructure Fund, a government-backed initiative launched in 2020 to reduce post-harvest crop loss and increase farmer income, has facilitated over ₹1.15 trillion in investment across 123,650 projects. The fund provides a 3% interest subvention and credit guarantee to eligible borrowers for loans up to ₹2 crore. To date, financial institutions have sanctioned ₹71,720 crore in loans, with ₹49,500 crore already disbursed. The projects, which include warehouses, processing units, and custom hiring centers for farm equipment, are primarily driven by farmers and agri-entrepreneurs and are most prevalent in states like Madhya Pradesh and Maharashtra.
